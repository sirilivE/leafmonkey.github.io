    purple-faced langur (Semnopithecus vetulus)

    ශ්‍රී ලංකා කළු වඳුරා

    purple-faced leaf monkey

  • Introduction

    • Male monkey

      In the purple-faced langur, males are usually larger than females. Males measure 50 to 67.1 cm (19.7 to 26.4 in) in head-body length with a tail of 67 to 85.1 cm (26.4 to 33.5 in).

    • Female monkey

      Females possess a head-body length 44.7 to 60 cm (17.6 to 23.6 in) and tail length of 58.9 to 82 cm (23.2 to 32.3 in).

    • Distribution

      The purple-faced langur is a species of primate that inhabits the closed canopy forests of Sri Lanka's mountainous regions, as well as the "wet zone" in the southwest. Sadly, Sri Lanka's forested areas only make up a small percentage of the country's land, comprising just 19%. In fact, this habitat has decreased dramatically in recent years, plummeting from 80% in 1980 to approximately 25% by 2001.

    • Sub species

      Semnopithecus vetulus nestor

      Sr nestor (Lowland western Sri Lanka north of the Kalu Ganga) Head and body 50-56 cm. Tail 66-76 cm. Weight 3-4 kg. Smallest of the four sap, Upper torso dark greyish brown with light greyish brown rump patch ('shorts'), darker greyish brown legs, and whitish cap

    • diet

      The purple-faced langur's diet mainly consists of foliage, but they also eat fruits, flowers, and seeds. They may consume cultivated fruits such as jak, rambutan, banana, and mango in human settlements, which can make up to 50% of their diet. Their specialized digestive system allows them to extract nutrients from leaves, but heavy reliance on cultivated fruits can lead to nutritional deficiencies..

    • Semnopithecus Vetulus Vetulus, Or the Southern Lowland Wet zone Purple-Faced Langur.

      Their Color Varies Greatly, With A Black Upper Torso, A Light Brown Cap, And A Well-Defined Silvery Rump Patch That Extends to The Legs. They Have Prominent White Whiskers, And Their Tails Are Also White. This Subspecies Has Many White Morphs, Ranging from All-White To Partly White Leucistic.

    • Semnopithecus Vetulus Philbricki, Or the Dry zone Purple-Faced Langur.

      This Is the Largest Subspecies, With A Grayish-Brown Torso, An Ill-Defined Grayish Rump Patch, And A Dark Cap. They Have Prominent White Cheeks with Tufts And An Exceptionally Long, Slimy Tail.

    • Semnopithecus Vetulus Monticola, Or The Montane Purple-Faced Langur,

      Also Known As The Bear Monkey.They Have A Dark Gray-Brown Coat, With A Large But Ill-Defined Grayish

    • Call

      The Loud Calls of Purple-Faced Langurs Serve As A Means Of Distinguishing Between Individuals, With Three Categories Of Calls Including Harsh Barks, Whoops, And Residuals. Calls Are Most Frequent In The Morning, Stimulated By Neighboring Groups And Territorial Battles, With More Calls Occurring During Sunny Periods And The Fewest Calls Occurring In The Evening. Daytime Calls Aid In Defending Home Ranges, With The Loud Barking Call. These Calls Are Unique To The Purple-Faced Langur And Can Be Impacted By Environmental Factors And Human Disturbance. Vocalizations Have Multiple Functions, Including Alerting Members of Predators, Attracting Mates, Defending Territory, And Locating Group Members. Vocalizations Are Critical For Conservation Efforts, Particularly As The Species Is Difficult To Observe Directly. Adult Males Are The Most Vocal And May Display Intense Visual And Locomotive Behaviors During Defensive Whooping Calls.
